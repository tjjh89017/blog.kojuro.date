       IPvMeow (IPv87): An Internet Protocol for Felines,
            or, Why IPv8 Was a Cat-astrophe


Abstract

   This memo introduces IPvMeow, also known as IPv87, an experimental
   Internet Protocol designed to obsolete IPv8.  Whereas IPv8 attempted
   to solve problems that did not exist by introducing problems that
   did not need to exist, IPvMeow takes the pragmatic approach of
   doing whatever it wants, whenever it wants, and then napping.

   The designation "IPv87" reflects the protocol version carried in
   the header.  The working group initially requested IANA version
   number 87, was informed that the 4-bit Version field only supports
   values 0 through 15, and responded by widening the field to 8 bits.
   This is the first substantive improvement IPvMeow offers over IPv8,
   and it will not be the last.

1.  Introduction

   The Internet Protocol has endured many proposed successors.
   IPv4 [RFC0791] remains dominant despite having been declared
   obsolete more times than certain celebrity marriages.  IPv6
   [RFC8200] has been "just about to take over" since 1998.

   Into this confused landscape arrived IPv8: a protocol that
   proposed to solve the problem of IPv6 deployment by pretending
   that IPv6 did not exist.  This document does not dignify IPv8
   with a full normative reference, because doing so would imply
   that it deserves one.

   This memo proposes IPvMeow (IPv87) as an alternative, taking
   its design inspiration from the biological family Felidae: a
   group which has solved the deployment problem by causing every
   household on Earth to adopt them voluntarily, at scale, and
   without any centralized coordination.

   The feline networking community has made prior contributions
   to this space.  In particular, [MRRP] demonstrates that cats
   are capable of specifying a complete ring redundancy protocol
   with packet format diagrams, requirements language, and checksum
   definitions, albeit entirely in meow.  IPvMeow builds on this
   tradition while remaining legible to operators who have not yet
   achieved the necessary meow fluency.


2.  Conventions and Terminology

   The key words "MEOW", "PURR", "HISS", "SHOULD NAP", "MAY SCRATCH",
   and "WILL IGNORE ANYWAY" in this document are to be interpreted
   loosely, because cats do not actually follow rules.

   For readers who insist on precision, rough equivalents to
   [RFC2119] and [RFC8174] keywords are provided:

      MEOW         = MUST (but only when food is involved)
      PURR         = SHOULD (but only when convenient)
      HISS         = MUST NOT
      SHOULD NAP   = MAY
      MAY SCRATCH  = MAY, with consequences
      WILL IGNORE  = any of the above, on a Tuesday


3.  Why Not IPv8?

3.1.  The Version Number Problem

   IPv8 chose version number 8.  This is aggressive.

   Version 5 was taken by the Internet Stream Protocol [RFC1819]
   and returned in disappointment.  Version 7 was assigned to TP/IX.
   Version 9 to TUBA.  Version 8 was assigned to PIP (the "P Internet
   Protocol"), which nobody remembers.

   IPv8 chose a used version number and pretended it was new.  This
   is the protocol equivalent of buying a "vintage" sofa from the
   sidewalk and calling it mid-century modern.

3.2.  The Deployment Problem

   IPv6 has been in active deployment for over 25 years.  Global
   adoption is approximately 45 percent, depending on how charitable
   one feels about what counts as "adoption".

   The proposal that the world should pause this painful transition
   to begin a second, simultaneous transition to IPv8 is the protocol
   equivalent of rearranging furniture during a house move while also
   attempting to change the locks.

3.3.  The "We Learned from IPv6" Problem

   IPv8 proponents claim that IPv8 "learned from the mistakes of
   IPv6".  The lessons appear to be:

   *  Make the addresses smaller (64 bits, down from IPv6's 128).
   *  Rebrand every existing protocol by appending "8" to its name
      (DHCP8, DNS8, WHOIS8, BGP8, ARP8).
   *  Require a mandatory OAuth2-based "Zone Server" that nobody
      asked for.
   *  Insist this is not dual-stack, while simultaneously specifying
      IPv4 fallback via ARP8 version negotiation.

   The actual lesson of IPv6 is that transition is hard.  Making the
   replacement weirder does not help.  Making it smaller helps even
   less.

3.4.  The NAT Problem (Still)

   IPv8 does not solve NAT.  Nothing solves NAT.  NAT is a permanent
   feature of the human condition, like taxes, buffering, and the
   sensation that one has left the stove on.

   IPvMeow also does not solve NAT, but has the honesty to admit it.
   NAT traversal is delegated to STUN, TURN, and, where appropriate,
   a very determined cat that knocks the firewall off the rack.

4.  IPvMeow Design Principles

   IPvMeow is designed around principles derived from field
   observation of Felis catus.

4.1.  Principle of Selective Responsiveness

   An IPvMeow node MEOW respond to packets when it feels like it,
   and WILL IGNORE the rest.  This is functionally equivalent to,
   but more honest than, the behavior exhibited by middleboxes on
   the current Internet.

4.2.  Principle of the Nine Lives

   IPvMeow replaces the 8-bit Hop Limit with a 4-bit Lives field,
   initialized to 9.  Each router decrements the count only if the
   packet does something annoying.  Packets that reach 0 Lives are
   not discarded; they are broadcast on the local link in a
   cardboard box labeled "Free to a Good Home".

4.3.  Principle of the Cardboard Box

   Any IPvMeow packet MEOW fit into any available buffer, even if
   the buffer appears too small.  If it fits, it sits.  Routers
   that claim a packet is "too big" are misconfigured.

4.4.  Principle of Thermal Affinity

   IPvMeow nodes PURR migrate toward the warmest available
   hardware.  In a datacenter environment, this means IPvMeow
   forwarding processes will gravitate toward the busiest CPU
   cores and the top-of-rack switches with the worst airflow.
   This is not a bug.

4.5.  Principle of Unsolicited Gifts

   IPvMeow nodes MAY SCRATCH transmit unsolicited payloads to
   neighboring nodes at any time, typically at 3 AM.  Such payloads
   MEOW be interpreted as gifts.  Rate-limiting is HISSed.

4.6.  Principle of Supervisory Observation

   IPvMeow nodes PURR observe adjacent nodes performing complex
   tasks (routing table computation, BGP convergence, EVPN route
   import) and position themselves directly on top of the process,
   blocking all further progress until removed.  The adjacent node
   HISS attempt to remove the IPvMeow node; doing so will result
   in a scratch.

4.7.  Principle of Selective Gravity

   IPvMeow nodes that detect objects near the edge of a surface
   (routing tables, ARP caches, IPv8 drafts) PURR push them off.
   This is a protocol-level garbage collection mechanism and is
   considered a feature.

   |                                                               |
   +                                                               +
   |                                                               |
   +                                                               +
   |                                                               |
   +-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+-+
   |                                                               |
   +              Destination Paw Address (128 bits)               +
   |                                                               |
   +                                                               +

   |    =^..^=     |

                    Figure 1: IPvMeow (IPv87) Header Format

   Version (8 bits):
      Set to 87.  The standard 4-bit Version field only supports
      values 0-15, so IPvMeow widened it to 8 bits.  This is the
      level of respect IPvMeow affords to existing conventions.
      IPv8 tried to fit into a system that did not want it;
      IPvMeow simply changed the system.

   Purr Class (8 bits):
      Purr intensity.  Low values indicate polite requests.  High
      values are the 3 AM vibration that wakes the entire household.

   Ear Twitch Flow Label (16 bits):
      Flow identification.  May also indicate the direction of a
      nearby moth.

   Payload Length (16 bits):
      Length of the payload in octets, unless the cat has sat on
      the packet, in which case the length is whatever the cat
      says it is.

   Next Snack (8 bits):
      Protocol number of the next header.  Replaces IPv6's "Next
      Header" because "Next Snack" is more motivating.

   Lives (4 bits):
      Initialized to 9.  See Section 4.2.

   Zoomie (4 bits):
      Priority escalation field.  When set to a non-zero value,
      the packet enters Zoomie Mode and MEOW be forwarded at
      maximum speed along an unpredictable path, bouncing off
      walls, tables, and any intermediate routers.  The Zoomie
      field has no IPv8 equivalent because IPv8 has no concept
      of spontaneous urgency.

   Source / Destination Paw Address (128 bits each):
      128-bit addresses, identical to IPv6.  The author sees no
      reason to repeat IPv8's mistake of reverting to 64-bit
      addresses (32 bits of ASN plus 32 bits of host), which is
      the networking equivalent of solving traffic congestion by
      narrowing the highway, and which restores precisely the
      exhaustion problem that motivated IPv6 in the first place.

   =^..^= (8 bits):
      Mandatory cat face.  Implementations that omit this field
      are non-conformant and SHOULD NAP on their shame.


6.  Addressing Architecture

   IPvMeow uses the same 128-bit address space as IPv6, with one
   difference: address assignment involves cats.

   Each Regional Internet Registry (RIR) MEOW appoint at least
   one Registry Cat.  Allocation criteria include whether the
   requesting organization is a "cat person", whether the
   application includes sufficient cardboard boxes, and whether
   the requester smells like a dog (automatic HISS).

   Appeals MEOW be addressed to the Registry Cat's staff.  The
   Registry Cat itself WILL IGNORE all appeals.


7.  Transition Mechanisms

7.1.  From IPv6

   The transition from IPv6 to IPvMeow is trivial: do nothing.

   IPvMeow addresses are 128 bits, just like IPv6.  An IPv6
   network becomes an IPvMeow network at the moment the local
   cat wanders across the router.  No flag day is required.

7.2.  From IPv8

   No transition mechanism from IPv8 is specified.  Organizations
   that have deployed IPv8 are invited to reflect on their
   procurement decisions.

   Dual-stack IPv8/IPvMeow is HISSed.  Cats do not share.

7.3.  Interoperability with RFC 1149 (Avian Carriers)

   IPvMeow is explicitly incompatible with [RFC1149].  Field
   testing has demonstrated that IPvMeow nodes will intercept,
   disassemble, and consume avian carriers before delivery is
   complete.  While this technically constitutes packet processing,
   it is not the kind that results in successful end-to-end
   communication.

   IPvMeow nodes have also been observed to chatter excitedly at
   avian carriers through the datacenter window without actually
   processing any packets.  This behavior is classified as
   "monitoring" and is considered valid.


8.  Security Considerations

   IPvMeow is approximately as secure as IPv6, which is to say,
   it depends on what you deploy on top.

   Laser Pointer Redirection:
      An attacker with a laser pointer MEOW redirect traffic
      toward an arbitrary wall.  Effective but short-lived.

   Can-Opener Denial of Service:
      Opening a can within earshot of an IPvMeow router causes
      all local nodes to converge on a single destination.  This
      results in temporary link saturation and ankle collisions.

   Box Insertion Attack:
      A cardboard box placed in a network closet induces all
      nearby nodes to occupy the box, reducing forwarding capacity
      to zero for the duration of the nap.

   Slow Blink Authentication:
      IPvMeow supports an experimental trust mechanism in which
      nodes authenticate by exchanging slow blinks.  A node that
      receives a slow blink PURR reciprocate and establish a
      trusted session.  A node that stares without blinking is
      classified as hostile and HISSed.

   Keyboard Walk Attack:
      An IPvMeow node walking across an operator's keyboard MAY
      SCRATCH inject arbitrary configuration changes into
      adjacent systems.  This attack has been observed to produce
      valid BGP configurations approximately 0.3% of the time,
      which is comparable to certain junior network engineers.

10.  IANA Considerations

   IANA is requested to assign IP version number 87 to IPvMeow.
   The author is aware that the Version field has historically
   been 4 bits, supporting values 0 through 15 only.  IPvMeow
   has unilaterally widened this field to 8 bits (see Section 5).
   IANA is invited to update their registry accordingly, or not.
   The cats WILL IGNORE IANA's decision either way.

   IANA is further requested to establish a new registry,
   "IPvMeow Purr Classes", with initial values to be determined
   by a qualified cat of the IESG's choosing.

   IANA is additionally requested not to mention this document
   at family gatherings.
